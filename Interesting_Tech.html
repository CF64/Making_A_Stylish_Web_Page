Self-Driving Cars

Self-Driving Cars are cars or trucks which require no human control to be safely operated. They are also known as autonomous or driverless cars. They combine sensors, and software to control, navigate and drive the vehicle. Self-Driving technology is being developed by Google, Nvidia, BMW, Mercedes, Uber, Tesla, other major automakers, research and technology companies. Even though the technology is still in its infancy, it is becoming increasingly common with companies like GM and Nissan planning to deploy Self-Driving cars to the public in 2019. Benefits of Self-Driving-Cars

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) list the folowing as benefits of Self-Driving Cars:

  • Safety

    94 percent of serious accident serious crashes are due to human error. Self-Driving Cars have the potential to remove human error from the equation and protect drivers and passengers, as well as bicyclist and pedestrians.

  • Economic and Societal Benefits

    A NHTSA study showed motor vehicle crashes in 2010 cost $242 billion in economic activity, including $57.6 billion in lost workplace productivity, and $594 billion due to loss of life and decreased quality of life due to injuries. Eliminating the vast majority of motor vehicle crashes could erase these costs.

  • Efficiency and Convenience

    Roads filled with automated vehicles could also cooperate to smooth traffic flow and reduce traffic congestion.

  • Mobility

    Automated vehicles may also provide new mobility options to millions more Americans. Today there are 49 million Americans over age 65 and 53 million people have some form of disability.

Artificial-Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence is the theory and development of computer systems able to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence, such as visual perception, speech recognition, decision-making, and translation between languages.The folowing video provides a good explanation of what Artificial Intelligence is Modern day examples of AI include:

Machine Learning
The science of getting computers to learn without programing.
Robotics
A field of engineering focused on the design and manufacturing of robots.
Machine Vision
The process of making computers see by capturing and analyzing visual information using a camera, analog-to-digital conversion and digital signal processing.
Natural Language Processing (NLP)
The processing of human language by a computer program. NLP tasks include text translation and speech recognition.
